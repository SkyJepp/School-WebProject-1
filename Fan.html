AVOIDING

By: Fan

Understanding the Avoiding Strategy

Avoiding is a strategy where you choose not to engage in conflict. It’s characterized by low concern for both your own needs and the needs of others. While this might sound counterproductive, it can be a smart move in specific situations. For example, suppose the issue is trivial or engaging in the conflict could cause more harm than good. In that case, avoiding it can prevent unnecessary drama and save time and emotional energy. But here’s the flip side: relying too much on Avoidance can lead to bigger problems. Unresolved conflicts don’t disappear; they fester. Over time, this can cause stress, harm relationships, and even lead to explosive confrontations. The key is knowing when to use Avoidance as a short-term strategy rather than a default response to every conflict.

The Avoidance strategy isn’t about running from conflict. It’s about knowing when stepping back is the smartest move. Use it sparingly and strategically for minor issues or when confrontation could lead to more harm than resolution. Just remember unresolved conflicts need to be revisited eventually, or they can grow into bigger problems down the line.

Example Scenario: Using Avoiding Wisely

"Picture this: You’re in a part of a workplace meeting, and a team member suddenly pushes for an idea you think is impractical. You know that challenging them at the moment might trigger a defensive response and derail the discussion. So, instead of voicing your concerns immediately, you choose to avoid the conflict for now. After the meeting, you quietly share your thoughts with the team leader, suggesting an alternative approach that aligns better with the group’s goals. In this case, Avoiding helps you sidestep unnecessary tension while still ensuring the issue gets addressed constructively."
