COMPETING

Understanding the Competing Strategy

By: Skylar

Competing is a style marked by strong assertiveness and minimal cooperation. People who use this approach are confident in their own views and reluctant to compromise to achieve their goals at the expense of other parties involved.  It is so often what most people call a win-lose situation Competing conflict style can be effective but should be used selectively. It's most appropriate in specific situations such as:

  • Crisis situations that require quick decision-making skills
  • When implementing essential but unpopular changes
  • Standing up for yourself against unfair treatment
  • Can create an environment to push individuals to do their very best producing good quality work

Understanding this style can help you learn when to pick your battles, practice assertive communication, and be aware of mistakes that potentially might escalate conflict.    The characteristics align with the model's framework where competing sits at the intersection of high assertiveness and low cooperation. While the style can be effective in specific situations, it represents the most confrontational approach in the Thomas-Kilmann model, potentially leading to relationship damage and conflict escalation if overused.

Example of a Competitive Conflict Style

Illustration of two people competing, one of the main conflict styles at work

Imagine two sprinters, Carrey and Alexis, representing the same country competing for gold medal for the 100m relay swimming race. Both have trained tirelessly and believe they have the potential to win. As they line up on starting block, their focus is solely on their own victory. They may engage in psychological games, trying to intimidate each other or gain a mental edge. This intense competition reflects the competing conflict style, where both athletes are highly assertive but unwilling to compromise their individual goals. In this situation, the athletes are prioritizing their personal goals, focusing on their own success rather than cooperating. Both are highly motivated to win the gold medal and are determined to achieve their individual goals.

They show high assertiveness by working hard in their training, receiving the best coaching, and standing up for themselves, explaining why they deserve to win for their country. However, they display low cooperativeness, as they are more focused on their own ambitions than helping each other. Instead of supporting one another, they communicate directly to express their needs and desires, even though they are on the same team. Since only one can win, this creates a competitive and potentially tense relationship between them.

While the competing style can be effective in certain situations, like urgent decisions or when strong leadership is needed, it can also lead to negative consequences like strained relationships and decreased cooperation in the long term. In this scenario, it's crucial for the athletes to maintain a healthy balance between competition and sportsmanship.
